Qin Jiushao (秦九韶) & The Roots of Algebra

Before computers, solving high-order polynomial equations was incredibly difficult. In 1247, the Chinese mathematician Qin Jiushao published the masterpiece Shu Shu Jiu Zhang (數書九章, Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections).

He introduced a method for solving numerical equations that is mathematically identical to what we learn today as "Synthetic Division" or Horner's Method. While William Horner rediscovered this in the West in 1819, Qin used this algorithmic approach (using counting rods) nearly 600 years earlier!

"When we perform synthetic division today to check factors, we are walking in the footsteps of counting rods laid out in the Song Dynasty."

數書九章

Shu Shu Jiu Zhang

Theorems

Remainder Theorem

If a polynomial \(f(x)\) is divided by \((x-a)\), the remainder is \(f(a)\).

Factor Theorem

If \(f(a) = 0\), then \((x-a)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).

Conversely, if \((x-a)\) is a factor, \(f(a)=0\).

Cubic Identities

Sum of Cubes

\(a^3 + b^3 \equiv (a+b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)\)

Difference of Cubes

\(a^3 - b^3 \equiv (a-b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)\)
