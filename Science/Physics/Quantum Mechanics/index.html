Fundamentals

What is Quantum Mechanics?

Classical physics explains the motion of planets and baseballs. Quantum mechanics explains the bizarre behavior of subatomic particles like electrons and photons.

Wave-Particle Duality

Matter and light exhibit the behaviors of both waves and particles. An electron can diffract like a wave but hit a detector like a particle.

Quantization

Energy comes in discrete packets called "quanta." An electron in an atom can only exist at specific energy levels, never in between.

Core Principles

Superposition

A system exists in all possible states simultaneously until it is measured. Think of Schrödinger's famous cat: both dead and alive until the box is opened.

Entanglement

"Spooky action at a distance." Two particles become linked such that the state of one instantly influences the state of the other, regardless of distance.

Uncertainty Principle

Formulated by Heisenberg, it states that you cannot know both the position and momentum of a particle with perfect precision. The more you know one, the less you know the other.

The Language of Quantum Mechanics

The state of a quantum system is described by a wavefunction, represented by the Greek letter Psi ($\Psi$). Its evolution over time is governed by the Schrödinger Equation.

$$ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r},t) = \hat{H} \Psi(\mathbf{r},t) $$

$$i$$ : Imaginary unit
$$\hbar$$ : Reduced Planck constant
$$\hat{H}$$ : Hamiltonian (Total Energy)
