What is Parity?

In quantum mechanics, **Parity ($P$)** describes the property of a physical system under spatial inversion—flipping coordinates $(x, y, z)$ to $(-x, -y, -z)$. Simply put, it is the symmetry of a mirror reflection.

Conservation of Parity (The Old Law):

If you watch a physics experiment in a mirror, the reflection should obey the same physical laws as the real world. This holds true for Gravity, Electromagnetism, and the Strong Nuclear Force.

The Lee & Yang Hypothesis

In 1956, physicists **T.D. Lee** and **C.N. Yang** were investigating the "Theta-Tau Puzzle." They made a bold proposal that shocked the community:

"Parity is not conserved in weak interactions."

This meant that in processes like $\beta$-decay, nature possesses a fundamental "chirality" (handedness).

Mathematical Formalism

The Parity operator $P$ acting on a wavefunction $\psi$:

$$ P\psi(\mathbf{r}) = \eta_P \psi(-\mathbf{r}) $$

If $|P\psi|^2 \neq |\psi|^2$ under certain interactions, parity is violated. This implies the Hamiltonian $H$ does not commute with $P$: $[H, P] \neq 0$.

The Wu Experiment (Simulation)

To test the hypothesis, experimentalist **Chien-Shiung Wu** designed a brilliant experiment. She cooled Cobalt-60 ($^{60}Co$) nuclei to near absolute zero ($0.01K$) and applied a strong magnetic field to align their spins.

What to observe:

  • Room Temp: Thermal energy disrupts alignment. Spins are random. Electrons are emitted equally in all directions (Isotropic).
  • Low Temp (The Discovery): The magnetic field aligns the nuclear spins (Up). If parity were conserved, electrons should go Up and Down equally. Instead, electrons preferentially shoot DOWN (opposite to the spin). This asymmetry proves Parity Violation.

Historical Impact

The results were stunning. The universe was not symmetric. In 1957, Lee and Yang were awarded the **Nobel Prize in Physics**—the quickest recognition in Nobel history (just one year after their paper).

Although Chien-Shiung Wu did not share the Nobel Prize—a point of historical controversy—her experimental validation is considered one of the most elegant and technically difficult in the history of physics. She is widely known as the "First Lady of Physics."
